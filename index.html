User-Centric BI Dashboards in University Student Affairs

Key Insights

  • Multi-level dashboard differentiation: strategic, tactical, operational
  • User-Centered Design principles critical for adoption
  • Application across admissions, advising, engagement, financial aid
  • AI and machine learning integration growing rapidly

Executive Summary

A comprehensive literature review on user-centric, multi-level BI dashboards for university student affairs from the last five years reveals significant advancements in theoretical frameworks, practical implementations, and usability studies.

Key Findings

  • • Clear differentiation of dashboard types across organizational hierarchy
  • • Critical role of User-Centered Design (UCD) principles in development
  • • Widespread application across student affairs functions
  • • Growing use of BI for student support and dropout prevention

Emerging Trends

  • • Increased adoption of AI and machine learning
  • • Focus on data-empowered institutions
  • • Personalized student experiences
  • • Advanced analytics integration

However, gaps remain in universally accepted implementation roadmaps and specific design considerations for strategic-level dashboards in student affairs. The research highlights the need for more holistic approaches that integrate technical, organizational, and human factors in BI adoption.

1. Theoretical Frameworks for Multi-Level BI Dashboards

1.1 Defining Strategic, Tactical, and Operational Dashboards

The conceptualization of Business Intelligence (BI) dashboards within Higher Education Institutions (HEIs) involves a hierarchical classification into strategic, tactical, and operational levels, a framework crucial for tailoring information presentation and analytical capabilities to specific user needs [4], [90].

Strategic

Senior executives focus on high-level KPIs aligned with long-term institutional goals

  • • Overall student enrollment
  • • Retention rates
  • • Graduation rates

Tactical

Mid-level managers focus on departmental processes and program effectiveness

  • • Departmental performance
  • • Resource allocation
  • • Initiative monitoring

Operational

Frontline staff focus on real-time day-to-day activities and processes

  • • Real-time alerts
  • • Student case status
  • • Immediate support

"Dashboards are a visual display of the most important information needed to achieve one or more objectives; consolidated and arranged on a single screen so the information can be monitored at a glance"

— [258]

Research by [258], [408] indicates that operational dashboards were the most prevalent in literature, followed by tactical, and then strategic, suggesting a potential research gap in strategic-level dashboard usage.

2. User-Centric Design in BI Dashboards

2.1 Principles of User-Centered Design

User-Centered Design (UCD) is an iterative design process that focuses on understanding and addressing the needs, preferences, and behaviors of end-users throughout the development lifecycle [254], [240].

Core UCD Principles

  • Understanding users and context: Deep analysis of user needs and work environment [191], [239]
  • Early and continuous involvement: Stakeholder engagement throughout development [215], [240]
  • Simplicity and ease of use: Intuitive interfaces and reduced complexity [191], [240]
  • Consistency: Standardized design patterns and interactions [239], [240]
  • Accessibility and inclusivity: Design for diverse user capabilities [239]
  • Feedback and iteration: Continuous improvement based on user input [239]

ISO 9241-210 Standard

Provides UCD methodology for interactive systems, emphasizing:

  1. Understanding user needs
  2. Specifying context of use
  3. Involving users throughout design and development [51], [353]
Research Evidence

Systematic review of 124 peer-reviewed articles (2010-2023) found that UCD approaches markedly enhance interface usability, reduce cognitive barriers, and improve engagement [44], [574].

5. Gaps and Future Research Directions

5.1 Identified Gaps in Current Research

Implementation Roadmaps

Lack of a universally accepted and comprehensive roadmap for implementing BI systems in HEIs [5], [474].

While various frameworks exist, a standardized, validated approach adaptable across diverse institutional contexts is needed.

Strategic-Level Dashboards

Insufficient research on strategic-level dashboards specifically for student affairs [258], [408].

Research by Rahman et al. (2017) found operational dashboards most discussed, with strategic dashboards receiving comparatively less attention.

User-Centric Design Specialization

Need for more specific research on tailoring UCD principles to unique requirements of different user groups within student affairs.

Systematic review highlighted insufficient exploration of inclusive design practices for underrepresented user groups [574].

Holistic Integration

Need for more holistic research integrating technical, organizational, and human factors influencing BI adoption.

Includes understanding socio-technical characteristics that influence user engagement and institutional contexts [294].

5.2 Roadmaps and Implementation Challenges

Key Implementation Challenges

Technical Challenges
  • Data Quality and Integration: Ensuring accurate, complete, reliable data from disparate sources [662]
  • BI Tool Selection: Appropriate technologies aligning with user needs and IT infrastructure [5], [62]
  • System Integration: Connecting SIS, LMS, financial systems into cohesive data warehouse [417], [667]
Organizational Challenges
  • Leadership Support: Securing commitment and fostering data-driven culture [5], [662]
  • Staff Capabilities: Ensuring BI expertise and data literacy among users
  • Change Management: Managing organizational transformation effectively

Kimball Methodology Approach

Bottom-up development strategy focusing on business-specific data marts aligned with user needs [376].

  • • Incremental development approach
  • • Adaptability to changing requirements
  • • Direct end-user engagement
  • • Reduced design-business misalignment

Validated Roadmap Example

UTAD case study validated roadmap emphasizing user-centric approach and continuous feedback [661].

  • • Expert interviews for requirements
  • • User group evaluations of dashboards
  • • Continuous feedback mechanisms
  • • Post-implementation adjustments

Conclusion

The literature review reveals significant advancements in user-centric BI dashboards for university student affairs, with clear differentiation between strategic, tactical, and operational levels. While substantial progress has been made in theoretical frameworks and practical implementations, gaps remain in comprehensive implementation roadmaps and strategic-level dashboard design.

The future points toward increased integration of AI, machine learning, and advanced analytics, moving beyond descriptive reporting to predictive and prescriptive insights. However, this evolution must be accompanied by robust ethical frameworks and responsible data governance to ensure equitable and transparent use of these powerful technologies.

Success in BI implementation requires a holistic approach that considers technical, organizational, and human factors, with user-centered design principles guiding development at every stage. The ultimate goal remains enhancing student success and institutional effectiveness through data-informed decision-making.
